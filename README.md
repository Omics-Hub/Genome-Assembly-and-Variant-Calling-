# Course Overview

Genome assembly is one of the fundamental tasks in modern genomics. It involves reconstructing the original genome of an organism from millions or billions of DNA sequencing reads generated by high-throughput sequencing technologies. Because sequencing instruments cannot read an entire chromosome in one continuous stretch, genomic DNA is fragmented into many smaller pieces that are sequenced independently. Computational algorithms are then used to reconstruct the original genome by identifying overlaps or shared sequence information among these fragments.

Genome assembly underpins nearly every downstream genomic analysis. A high-quality genome assembly enables researchers to identify genes, characterize structural variation, investigate evolutionary relationships, annotate regulatory elements, compare genomes across species, and study genome organization. The quality of the assembly directly influences the accuracy of these downstream analyses.

This course introduces the biological and computational principles of genome assembly, with a focus on de novo assembly using Illumina short-read sequencing data. Students will learn the theory behind assembly algorithms and gain practical experience assembling, evaluating, and interpreting genome assemblies.

# Module 1 – Introduction to Genomes

## What is a Genome?

A genome is the complete genetic material of an organism. It contains all of the DNA sequences required for the development, maintenance, reproduction, and function of that organism. Depending on the organism, the genome may consist of a single circular chromosome (as in many bacteria) or multiple linear chromosomes (as in plants, animals, and fungi).

A genome contains much more than protein-coding genes. It includes:

- Protein-coding genes
- Regulatory elements
- Introns
- Intergenic regions
- Ribosomal RNA genes
- Transfer RNA genes
- Repetitive DNA
- Transposable elements
- Structural DNA such as centromeres and telomeres

Although genes often receive the most attention, they constitute only a small fraction of many eukaryotic genomes. For example, protein-coding genes account for less than 2% of the human genome.

---

## Genome Size

Genome size varies dramatically among organisms and does not necessarily correlate with organismal complexity, a phenomenon known as the **C-value paradox**.

| Organism | Approximate Genome Size |
|-----------|------------------------|
| *Mycoplasma genitalium* | 580 kb |
| *Escherichia coli* | 4.6 Mb |
| *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* | 12 Mb |
| *Arabidopsis thaliana* | 135 Mb |
| Rice | 430 Mb |
| Zebrafish | 1.5 Gb |
| Human | 3.2 Gb |
| Wheat | 17 Gb |

Large genomes often contain extensive repetitive DNA rather than proportionally more genes.

# Module 2 – DNA Sequencing Technologies

Genome assembly begins with DNA sequencing. Different sequencing technologies generate reads that differ in length, accuracy, throughput, and cost.

---

## Sanger Sequencing

Sanger sequencing, developed in 1977, was the first widely adopted DNA sequencing technology and laid the foundation for genomics.

### Principle

DNA polymerase extends a growing DNA strand until a fluorescently labeled dideoxynucleotide (ddNTP) is incorporated. Because ddNTPs lack a 3′ hydroxyl group, DNA synthesis terminates. By separating fragments of different lengths and detecting the fluorescent label, the DNA sequence can be reconstructed.

### Characteristics

| Feature | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Read length | 700–1000 bp |
| Accuracy | >99.99% |
| Throughput | Low |
| Cost per base | High |

### Advantages

- Extremely accurate
- Long reads relative to first-generation technologies
- Gold standard for validation

### Limitations

- Expensive
- Low throughput
- Not suitable for whole-genome sequencing

---

## Illumina Sequencing

Illumina sequencing is currently the most widely used platform for genome sequencing.

### Sequencing by Synthesis

DNA fragments are attached to a flow cell and amplified to form clusters. During sequencing, fluorescently labeled nucleotides are incorporated one base at a time. After each incorporation, the instrument captures an image to identify the incorporated nucleotide.

This process is repeated for hundreds of cycles.

### Characteristics

| Feature | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Read length | 75–300 bp |
| Accuracy | >99.9% |
| Throughput | Very high |
| Cost | Low |

### Advantages

- Very accurate
- High throughput
- Low cost per base
- Ideal for population-scale sequencing

### Limitations

- Short read length
- Difficulty resolving repetitive regions
- Challenging structural variant detection

---

## Pacific Biosciences (PacBio)

PacBio sequencing generates long reads by observing DNA polymerase activity in real time.

Modern HiFi sequencing produces long reads with very high accuracy.

| Feature | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Read length | 10–25 kb |
| Accuracy | >99.8% (HiFi) |

### Advantages

- Resolves repeats
- Excellent for de novo assembly
- Accurate structural variant detection

### Limitations

- Higher sequencing cost
- Requires high-quality DNA

---

## Oxford Nanopore Sequencing

Nanopore sequencing measures changes in electrical current as DNA molecules pass through nanopores.

Unlike other technologies, there is theoretically no upper limit on read length.

| Feature | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Read length | Up to several Mb |
| Accuracy | Platform dependent |

### Advantages

- Ultra-long reads
- Real-time sequencing
- Portable instruments
- Direct RNA sequencing

### Limitations

- Lower raw accuracy than Illumina
- Requires computational polishing

---

## Comparison of Sequencing Platforms

| Technology | Read Length | Accuracy | Best Application |
|------------|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Sanger | ~1000 bp | Excellent | Validation |
| Illumina | 150 bp | Excellent | Short-read assembly |
| PacBio HiFi | 15–25 kb | Excellent | Reference genomes |
| Nanopore | >100 kb | Good | Structural variation |

# Module 3 – Library Preparation

Before sequencing can begin, genomic DNA must be converted into a sequencing library.

A sequencing library is a collection of DNA fragments that have been prepared for sequencing by adding platform-specific adapters.

---

## DNA Extraction

High-quality DNA is essential for successful sequencing.

Poor-quality DNA results in:

- fragmented reads
- uneven coverage
- assembly errors

Important quality measurements include:

- DNA concentration
- Purity (A260/A280)
- Integrity
- Fragment size

---

## DNA Fragmentation

Because sequencing instruments cannot sequence intact chromosomes, DNA is fragmented.

Methods include:

- Sonication
- Enzymatic digestion
- Mechanical shearing

Fragment size depends on the sequencing platform.

---

## Adapter Ligation

Synthetic DNA adapters are ligated to both ends of each DNA fragment.

Adapters allow:

- PCR amplification
- Cluster generation
- Primer binding
- Sample indexing

---

## PCR Amplification

Most sequencing workflows amplify the library before sequencing.

PCR increases DNA quantity but may introduce:

- amplification bias
- duplicate reads
- GC bias

PCR-free libraries are preferred when sufficient DNA is available.

# Module 4 – Sequencing Concepts

## Read

A read is the nucleotide sequence generated from a single DNA fragment during sequencing.

Example:

```
ATCGATCGATCGATCG
```

Each sequencing experiment generates millions to billions of reads.

---

## Single-End Reads

Only one end of the DNA fragment is sequenced.

```
-------------->
```

Advantages

- Lower cost
- Simpler analysis

Limitations

- Less information for assembly

---

## Paired-End Reads

Both ends of the DNA fragment are sequenced.

```
---------->
<----------
```

The approximate distance between the reads (insert size) is known.

Advantages

- Better repeat resolution
- Improved scaffolding
- More accurate assemblies

Most Illumina genome assembly projects use paired-end sequencing.

---

## Insert Size

The insert size is the length of the DNA fragment between sequencing adapters.

```
Read1 ----->

=================

<----- Read2
```

Insert size affects the ability to bridge repeats and scaffold contigs.

---

## Sequencing Coverage

Coverage (or depth) describes how many times, on average, each nucleotide in the genome has been sequenced.

The expected coverage is calculated as:

\[
C=\frac{N\times L}{G}
\]

where:

- **N** = number of reads
- **L** = average read length
- **G** = genome size

Example:

- 100 million reads
- 150 bp/read
- Genome size = 500 Mb

Coverage:

\[
\frac{100,000,000 \times 150}{500,000,000}=30\times
\]

Coverage strongly influences assembly quality.

### Low Coverage

- Missing regions
- Fragmented assembly
- Low confidence

### Excessively High Coverage

- Increased computational cost
- Greater representation of sequencing errors
- Diminishing returns beyond a certain depth

Typical recommendations:

| Coverage | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| 20–30× | Small bacterial genomes |
| 30–60× | Most eukaryotic genomes |
| 60–100× | Complex genomes and polishing |

# Module 5 – Sequencing File Formats

## FASTQ

The FASTQ format stores sequencing reads and their corresponding quality scores.

Each read occupies four lines:

```text
@Read001
ATCGATCGATCG
+
FFFFFFFFFFFF
```

The four lines represent:

1. Read identifier
2. DNA sequence
3. Separator (`+`)
4. Phred quality scores

---

## Phred Quality Scores

Phred scores estimate the probability that a base has been called incorrectly.

| Q Score | Error Probability | Accuracy |
|----------|-------------------|----------|
| Q20 | 1 in 100 | 99% |
| Q30 | 1 in 1000 | 99.9% |
| Q40 | 1 in 10000 | 99.99% |

Higher scores indicate greater confidence in the base call.

# Module 6 – Introduction to Genome Assembly

## Why is Genome Assembly Necessary?

Current sequencing technologies generate short DNA fragments rather than complete chromosomes. Genome assembly reconstructs the original genome by identifying how these fragments relate to one another.

The process can be visualized as solving a jigsaw puzzle without knowing the final image. Each sequencing read represents a puzzle piece, and the assembler attempts to determine how the pieces fit together based on shared sequence information.

```text
Genome
──────────────────────────────────────

↓

Fragmentation

ATCGATCG
     GATCGAAA
          CGAAATTT
               AATTTCCC

↓

Assembly

ATCGATCGAAATTTCCC
```

Assembly algorithms use overlaps, shared k-mers, or graph-based methods to reconstruct longer contiguous sequences from these reads.

The quality of the final assembly depends on:

- Read quality
- Sequencing depth
- Genome complexity
- Repeat content
- Choice of assembly algorithm

---

## Types of Genome Assembly

### Reference-Guided Assembly

Reads are aligned to an existing reference genome.

Advantages:

- Faster
- Computationally efficient
- Accurate for closely related organisms

Disadvantages:

- Unable to recover novel genomic regions
- Biased toward the reference genome

---

### De Novo Assembly

Reads are assembled without using a reference genome.

Advantages:

- Detects novel sequences
- Suitable for new species
- Produces independent genome assemblies

Disadvantages:

- Computationally intensive
- More sensitive to sequencing errors and repetitive DNA

# Module 7 – Fundamental Assembly Terminology

## Contig

A contig is a continuous DNA sequence reconstructed solely from overlapping sequencing reads. No gaps exist within a contig, meaning every nucleotide is directly supported by sequencing evidence.

```text
Read 1   ACTGATCG
Read 2      GATCGAAA
Read 3          CGAAATTT

↓

Contig

ACTGATCGAAATTT
```

Contigs end when the assembler can no longer confidently determine the next nucleotide, often due to repetitive DNA or insufficient coverage.

---

## Scaffold

A scaffold links multiple contigs together using additional information, such as paired-end reads, mate-pair libraries, long reads, or Hi-C data.

Unknown regions between contigs are represented by `N` characters.

```text
Contig A
──────────────

NNNNNNNNNN

Contig B
──────────────────────
```

Scaffolds provide a higher level of genome organization than contigs but may still contain unresolved gaps.

---

## Gap

A gap is an unresolved region of the genome where the approximate location is known but the exact nucleotide sequence has not been determined.

Gaps are represented by:

```text
NNNNNNNNNNNNNNN
```

---

## Assembly Continuity

Assembly continuity describes how fragmented or contiguous an assembly is.

Highly contiguous assemblies contain fewer, longer contigs and scaffolds, while fragmented assemblies consist of many short contigs.

Improving continuity is a major goal of genome assembly because longer assembled sequences simplify downstream analyses such as gene prediction and structural variant detection.
